## Station 1: What is computer science?

**Krugo:**

Welcome to our first station! Before we examine computers more closely, we
need to understand what computer science actually means.

Many people first think of computers, keyboards or programming. But computer
science is about much more. It studies how information can be represented,
stored, processed and transmitted. A computer is one of its most important
tools.

Information is everywhere: your name on a ticket, the time on a departure
board or the destination of our InfoTrain. Computer scientists find useful
ways to organize this information and enable machines to work with it.

Computer science is also about solving problems. We divide a large problem
into smaller steps and describe an unambiguous solution. We will use this way
of thinking throughout our journey.

**InfoTrain-Beispiel:**

For our train to operate, its destination, departure time, passengers and
available seats must be managed. Computer science helps organize all of this
information and use it to make the right decisions.

**Übergang:**

Now we know what computer science is about. Next, we will see how a computer
processes information.

## Station 2: The IPO model

**Krugo:**

Almost every computer system works through three basic stages: input,
processing and output. Together they form the IPO model.

During input, a computer receives data. This may happen through a keyboard,
mouse, microphone, camera or sensor.

Processing comes next. The computer follows instructions, compares values or
calculates a result. It does not think like a person; it follows predefined
rules very quickly and precisely.

Finally, the system produces output. A result may appear on a screen, play
through a speaker or be sent to another device.

**InfoTrain-Beispiel:**

You select a destination at a ticket machine. That is the input. The machine
calculates the fare, which is processing. It then displays the price and
prints your ticket as output.

**Übergang:**

IPO describes the journey through a computer system. But what exactly travels
through it? To answer that, we need to distinguish data from information.

---

## Station 3: Data and information

**Krugo:**

Data consists of individual characters, numbers, pictures or measurements. On
its own, data may not have a clear meaning. It becomes information when we
place it in a meaningful context.

The number 14 is only a value at first. Next to the word "platform", it means
platform 14. Next to a clock, it may mean 2 p.m. Context determines which
information we understand.

Computers store and process data. People give it meaning. Data therefore needs
clear descriptions and useful organization.

There are many kinds of data: text, numbers, images, audio, video and location
data. Although they look very different to us, computers ultimately represent
all of them using numbers.

**InfoTrain-Beispiel:**

"10:32", "platform 4" and "Berlin" are separate pieces of data. Together they
form the information that the train to Berlin departs from platform 4 at
10:32.

**Übergang:**

Computers represent every kind of data using only two states. Our next station
shows how zeros and ones can create text, images and entire digital worlds.

---

## Station 4: Bits and binary numbers

**Krugo:**

Inside a computer are tiny electronic switches. In a simplified model, each
switch is either off or on. We represent these states with 0 and 1.

A single 0 or 1 is called a bit. One bit can store only two different states,
but several bits together can represent many values.

Eight bits usually form one byte. A byte can produce 256 different bit
patterns. These patterns may represent numbers, letters or color values.

Our familiar decimal system uses ten digits from 0 to 9. The binary system
uses only 0 and 1, yet it can still represent every number. For example, the
binary number 101 equals the decimal number 5.

**InfoTrain-Beispiel:**

A railway signal has two basic states: stop or proceed. That resembles one
bit. By combining many simple decisions like this, a system can control an
entire railway network.

**Übergang:**

We can now represent data. To do something useful with it, a computer needs a
precise sequence of instructions: an algorithm.

---

## Station 5: Algorithms and instructions

**Krugo:**

An algorithm is an unambiguous sequence of steps that solves a problem or
reaches a goal.

You already use algorithms in daily life. A recipe says which ingredients to
process and in which order. Directions explain step by step how to reach a
destination.

Instructions for a computer must be especially precise. "Move a little
farther" is unclear, while "move exactly 100 meters straight ahead" is
unambiguous. A computer does not fill in missing information through
experience or intuition.

A good algorithm finishes after a limited number of steps and produces a
reproducible result for valid inputs.

**InfoTrain-Beispiel:**

To reach the next station, the InfoTrain checks the signal, closes its doors,
accelerates to the permitted speed and brakes before the platform. The order
of these steps matters.

**Übergang:**

Algorithms describe what should happen. Now we will examine the computer
components that carry out these instructions.

## Station 6: Hardware and software

**Krugo:**

Hardware means every physical computer component you can touch. Examples
include the screen, keyboard, processor, memory and storage drive.

Software consists of programs and their data. Operating systems, browsers,
games and learning apps are all examples of software.

Hardware and software need each other. Without software, hardware does not
know which tasks to perform. Without hardware, software cannot run.

The processor executes instructions. Main memory keeps currently needed data
ready. Permanent storage preserves programs and files even after the device is
switched off.

**InfoTrain-Beispiel:**

The locomotive, wheels, doors and sensors are our train's hardware. The
program controlling its speed, displays and doors is its software.

**Übergang:**

Part of the hardware stores our data. At the next station, we will learn why
different kinds of memory exist and how files create order.

---

## Station 7: Storage and files

**Krugo:**

Computers need storage because data is needed at different times. Some data is
required only briefly, while other data should still be available tomorrow.

Main memory is very fast. It contains programs and data that the computer is
currently using. Its contents are normally lost when the device is switched
off.

An SSD or hard drive stores data permanently. Photos, documents, programs and
saved games can remain there.

Files group related data together, and folders help organize files. File names
and extensions provide clues about content and format, such as `.jpg` for an
image or `.txt` for text.

**InfoTrain-Beispiel:**

The train needs its current speed only during the journey. Its timetable and
maintenance reports, however, must be stored permanently.

**Übergang:**

Stored data becomes even more useful when devices can exchange it. Our journey
therefore continues into the world of networks.

---

## Station 8: Networks and the internet

**Krugo:**

A network connects several devices so that they can exchange data and use
shared services.

At home, a router often connects smartphones, computers, televisions and
other devices. Data is divided into small packets and transmitted through
cables or wireless signals.

The internet is a worldwide network made of many smaller networks. Servers
provide websites, messages, videos and other services. Your device requests
this data as a client.

An internet address helps data find the correct destination. Protocols define
the rules devices follow when communicating.

**InfoTrain-Beispiel:**

Stations, trains and control centers also form a network. They exchange
delays, platform changes and location data so everyone has the same current
information.

**Übergang:**

When data travels among many devices, we must protect it. Our next station is
therefore about security and privacy.

---

## Station 9: Security and privacy

**Krugo:**

IT security protects devices, programs and data against damage, loss and
unauthorized access.

A good password is long, unique and difficult to guess. Different accounts
should use different passwords. Two-factor authentication adds another layer
of protection.

Not every message or link is trustworthy. In phishing attacks, criminals try
to persuade people to reveal passwords or other personal information.

Privacy means that people can control how their personal data is used. You
should therefore consider which information you share and who is allowed to
see it.

**InfoTrain-Beispiel:**

A ticket may need to be checked, but your personal travel details should not
be visible to everyone. Only authorized people and systems should have access.

**Übergang:**

We now know the major building blocks of computer science. At the final
station, we will combine them into a small system of our own.

## Station 10: Final project – A digital InfoTrain

**Krugo:**

Welcome to the final basics station! We will now combine our discoveries and
design a digital ticket machine.

First, we define the inputs: a passenger selects a starting point,
destination and number of tickets. Next comes processing: the system validates
the input, finds a connection and calculates the fare. Finally, it produces
output by displaying the route, departure time, price and ticket.

The data must be represented clearly and some of it stored permanently.
Hardware receives the input and displays results. Software executes our
algorithm. A network can provide current timetable data.

Security and privacy are also part of the design. Payment details and personal
information must be processed securely.

A digital system is not built from one idea alone. Many areas of computer
science work together, which is why every station on our journey mattered.
